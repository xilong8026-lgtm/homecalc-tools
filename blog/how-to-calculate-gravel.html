Gravel Calculator: How Much Gravel for Driveway?

📅 June 20, 2026 | ⏱️ 10 min read | 🏷️ Gravel Calculator

Planning a gravel driveway, patio, or landscaping project? One of the most common mistakes DIYers make is ordering too little or too much gravel. Too little means you'll run out mid-project and pay extra for small deliveries. Too much means wasted money and a pile of excess gravel taking up space in your yard.

In this guide, you'll learn exactly how to calculate gravel needed for any project. We'll cover measuring your area, choosing the right depth, understanding compaction factors, and estimating costs. Plus, we'll show you how to use our free gravel calculator to get instant, accurate estimates.

Why Accurate Gravel Estimation Matters

Grave is typically sold by the ton or cubic yard, and delivery fees can be expensive ($50-150 per delivery). Ordering the wrong amount leads to:

  • Multiple small deliveries: Each delivery incurs a fee, quickly adding up.
  • Wasted material: Excess gravel is hard to return (most suppliers don't accept returns).
  • Project delays: Running out of material means waiting for a second delivery.
  • Uneven coverage: Too little gravel results in thin spots that shift under weight.
💡 Pro Tip: Always order 5-10% extra to account for compaction and uneven substrate. It's cheaper to have a little leftover than to order a second delivery.

Understanding Gravel Measurements

Before calculating, you need to understand how gravel is measured and sold:

1. Cubic Yards vs Tons

Grave is sold both by volume (cubic yards) and by weight (tons). Which one your supplier uses depends on the type of gravel:

Gravel Type Typical Unit Conversion (approx.)
Crushed stone (#57, #67) Tons 1 ton ≈ 0.75-0.85 cubic yards
Pea gravel Cubic yards or tons 1 cubic yard ≈ 1.2-1.4 tons
Decomposed granite Tons 1 ton ≈ 0.7-0.8 cubic yards
River rock Cubic yards 1 cubic yard ≈ 1.3-1.5 tons

Why the difference? Different types of gravel have different densities. Crushed stone is heavier (more compact) than pea gravel or river rock.

⚠️ Important: Always ask your supplier which unit they use and what the exact conversion factor is. Using the wrong conversion can lead to ordering 20-30% too much or too little!

2. Coverage Area

A cubic yard of gravel covers:

  • 2" deep: ≈ 162 square feet
  • 3" deep: ≈ 108 square feet
  • 4" deep: ≈ 81 square feet
  • 6" deep: ≈ 54 square feet

Example: If you have a 10'×20' driveway (200 sq ft) and want 4" depth, you'll need:

200 sq ft ÷ 81 sq ft/cubic yard = 2.47 cubic yards

Add 10% extra = 2.7 cubic yards

Step-by-Step Gravel Calculation

Step 1: Measure Your Area

Use a tape measure to find the length and width of your project area. For irregular shapes:

  • L-shaped driveway: Break into two rectangles, calculate separately, then add.
  • Circular area: Measure the radius (distance from center to edge), then use: Area = π × radius²
  • Triangular area: Use: Area = (base × height) ÷ 2

Example (rectangular driveway):

  • Length = 30 feet
  • Width = 12 feet
  • Area = 30 × 12 = 360 square feet

Step 2: Determine Required Depth

The right depth depends on your project type and the gravel's intended use:

Project Type Recommended Depth Reason
Walkway (light foot traffic) 2-3 inches Prevent sinking, ensure stability
Driveway (cars, trucks) 4-6 inches Support heavy weight, prevent ruts
Patio (under pavers) 3-4 inches Provide base, ensure drainage
Landscaping (decorative) 1-2 inches Aesthetic, minimal functional need
Shed/base foundation 4-8 inches Maximum stability, prevent settling
💡 Pro Tip: For driveways, use a 3-layer approach: 4" of crushed stone (base) + 2" of smaller stone (middle) + 1-2" of pea gravel or decorative stone (top). This provides better drainage and stability than a single layer.

Step 3: Calculate Volume

Now calculate the volume of gravel needed:

Formula: Volume (cubic feet) = Area (sq ft) × Depth (inches) ÷ 12

Then convert to cubic yards: Volume (cubic yards) = Volume (cubic feet) ÷ 27

Example (driveway):

  • Area = 360 sq ft
  • Depth = 4 inches = 4 ÷ 12 = 0.33 feet
  • Volume = 360 × 0.33 = 119.6 cubic feet
  • Volume = 119.6 ÷ 27 = 4.43 cubic yards

Step 4: Account for Compaction

Gravel compacts down when rolled or driven on. The amount of compaction depends on the gravel type:

Gravel Type Compaction Factor Explanation
Crushed stone (angular) 10-15% Angular pieces lock together, less compaction
Pea gravel (round) 15-20% Round stones shift more, more compaction
Decomposed granite 20-25% Fine particles compact significantly

Adjusted volume formula: Order_volume = Calculated_volume × (1 + Compaction_factor)

Example (crushed stone):

  • Calculated volume = 4.43 cubic yards
  • Compaction factor = 15% = 0.15
  • Order volume = 4.43 × 1.15 = 5.09 cubic yards

Step 5: Convert to Weight (if needed)

If your supplier sells by weight (tons), you'll need to convert:

Formula: Weight (tons) = Volume (cubic yards) × Conversion_factor

Typical conversion factors:

  • Crushed stone: 1 cubic yard ≈ 1.2-1.4 tons
  • Pea gravel: 1 cubic yard ≈ 1.3-1.5 tons
  • Decomposed granite: 1 cubic yard ≈ 1.4-1.6 tons

Example:

  • Volume = 5.09 cubic yards
  • Gravel type = Crushed stone (1.3 tons/cubic yard)
  • Weight = 5.09 × 1.3 = 6.62 tons
⚠️ Important: Always confirm the exact conversion factor with your supplier. It can vary based on moisture content and exact stone size.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. Ignoring Compaction

Many DIYers order exactly the calculated volume, then wonder why they're short after compaction. Always add 10-20% extra.

2. Choosing the Wrong Depth

Using 2" of gravel for a driveway will result in ruts and sinking within months. Follow the depth recommendations in the table above.

3. Not Preparing the Base

Gravel should be installed on a compacted soil base (or geotextile fabric). Skipping this step leads to uneven settling and potholes.

4. Forgetting About Edging

Without landscape edging, gravel will spread into your lawn or garden. Install rigid edging (plastic, metal, or wood) to contain the gravel.

5. Ordering Exact Amount (No Buffer)

Always order 5-10% more than calculated. It's better to have a little leftover than to pay for a second delivery.

💡 Pro Tip: If you have leftover gravel, use it for other projects (garden paths, drainage ditches) or offer it on local Facebook Marketplace/Craigslist. Many people are happy to take free gravel!

FAQ - Gravel Calculator

How deep should a gravel driveway be?

A gravel driveway should be 4-6 inches deep total, preferably in 2-3 layers. Use 4" of crushed stone (base) + 2" of smaller stone (middle). This provides better stability and drainage than a single 6" layer.

How much gravel do I need for a 10×20 patio?

For a 10×20 patio (200 sq ft) with 3" depth:

  • Volume = 200 × (3÷12) = 50 cubic feet
  • Volume = 50 ÷ 27 = 1.85 cubic yards
  • With 15% compaction = 2.13 cubic yards
Should I use landscape fabric under gravel?

Yes, highly recommended! Geotextile fabric prevents weeds from growing through, stabilizes the base, and prevents gravel from sinking into soil. It costs $20-50 per roll and saves hours of maintenance.

How do I calculate tons instead of cubic yards?

Use this formula: Weight (tons) = Volume (cubic yards) × Conversion_factor. The conversion factor depends on gravel type: crushed stone ≈ 1.3 tons/cubic yard, pea gravel ≈ 1.4 tons/cubic yard. Always confirm with your supplier!

Can I install gravel myself or should I hire a contractor?

For small projects (walkways, small patios), DIY is feasible. For large driveways, consider hiring a contractor for excavation and base preparation (requires heavy equipment). You can save money by spreading the gravel yourself after they prepare the base.

  • ✅ Choose depth (4-6" for driveways, 2-3" for walkways)

  • ✅ Add compaction factor (10-20%)
  • ✅ Convert to weight if needed (cubic yards × conversion factor)

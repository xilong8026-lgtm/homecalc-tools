Fence Calculator: Estimate Posts, Rails & Pickets

📅 July 11, 2026 | ⏱️ 12 min read | 🏷️ Fence Calculator

Building a fence is one of the most rewarding DIY projects you can tackle. But before you pick up a post hole digger, you need to answer one critical question: how much material do I need? Order too little, and you'll stall mid-project. Order too much, and you've wasted hundreds of dollars on extra posts and pickets you won't use.

In this guide, you'll learn exactly how to calculate fence materials — posts, rails, pickets, and concrete — for any fence style and length. We'll cover spacing rules, material selection, and cost estimation. Plus, we'll show you how to use our free fence calculator to get instant, accurate estimates.

Why Accurate Fence Material Calculation Matters

Fence materials are expensive. A typical 100-foot wood fence costs $1,500-3,000 in materials alone. Miscalculating by even 10% means:

  • Extra trips to the hardware store: Wasting time and gas on multiple supply runs.
  • Material waste: Most stores don't accept returns on cut lumber or bulk concrete.
  • Project delays: Running out of posts halfway through means waiting days to continue.
  • Visible inconsistencies: Mixing materials from different batches can cause color and texture mismatches.
💡 Pro Tip: Always buy 5-10% extra material to account for mistakes, warped boards, and future repairs. Store extras in a dry place — they'll save you a trip later when a picket gets damaged.

Understanding Fence Components

Before calculating, let's break down the four main components of a typical wood fence:

1. Fence Posts

Posts are the vertical structural supports that anchor your fence to the ground. They're set in concrete and bear the weight of the entire fence. Common types:

  • 4×4 posts: Standard for 4-6 ft fences (most common)
  • 6×6 posts: For 6-8 ft fences or high-wind areas
  • Metal posts: Galvanized steel, more durable but more expensive

2. Fence Rails (Stringers)

Rails are the horizontal beams that connect posts and support the pickets. Most fences use 2-3 rails per section:

  • 2 rails: For fences under 4 ft tall
  • 3 rails: For fences 4-6 ft tall (standard)
  • 4 rails: For fences over 6 ft or privacy fences with small gaps

Typical rail size: 2×4 lumber (actual: 1.5" × 3.5")

3. Fence Pickets

Pickets are the vertical boards that form the visible face of the fence. Spacing depends on your desired style:

  • Privacy fence: Pickets butted together (no gap) or with 1/4" gap
  • Semi-privacy: 1-2 inch gaps between pickets
  • Ranch/Rail style: 2-4 horizontal rails only, no vertical pickets

Typical picket sizes: 1×4, 1×6, or 1×8 boards (6 or 8 ft tall)

4. Concrete

Concrete anchors each post securely. Each post hole needs 1-3 bags of concrete depending on hole depth and diameter.

Step-by-Step Fence Material Calculation

Step 1: Measure Total Fence Length

Measure the total perimeter where the fence will be installed. For multi-sided fences (like a backyard enclosure), measure each side and add them together.

Example (rectangular backyard):

  • Side 1 (back): 80 feet
  • Side 2 (left): 50 feet
  • Side 3 (front): 80 feet
  • Side 4 (right): 50 feet (minus 4 ft gate = 46 feet)
  • Total fence length = 256 feet
⚠️ Important: Don't forget to account for gates! Subtract the gate width from your total fence length before calculating pickets. Gates need their own hardware (hinges, latches) and typically use a wider post.

Step 2: Calculate Number of Posts

Post spacing is the key factor. Standard spacing is 6-8 feet between posts:

Fence Type Post Spacing Why
Wood privacy fence (6 ft) 8 ft Standard, balances cost and strength
Wood privacy (high wind) 6 ft Closer spacing resists wind load
Chain link fence 10 ft Lighter material, wider spacing OK
Vinyl fence 6-8 ft Follow manufacturer specs
Aluminum/Steel fence 6-8 ft Standard panels come in 6-8 ft widths

Formula for posts:

Number of posts = (Total length ÷ Post spacing) + 1

Example (256 ft fence, 8 ft spacing):

  • 256 ÷ 8 = 32 sections
  • 32 + 1 = 33 posts
  • Add 2 extra posts for gate ends = 35 posts total

Step 3: Calculate Number of Rails

Rails run horizontally between posts. Each section needs one rail per level.

Formula for rails:

Number of rails = Number of sections × Rails per section

Example (33 sections, 3 rails each):

  • 33 × 3 = 99 rails
  • Rail length needed: 33 sections × 8 ft = 264 ft per rail level
  • Total rail lumber: 264 × 3 levels = 792 linear feet of 2×4
💡 Pro Tip: Buy rails in 16 ft lengths and cut them in half — this way each 16 ft board covers two 8 ft sections, minimizing waste and cuts.

Step 4: Calculate Number of Pickets

Picket calculation depends on picket width and gap spacing:

Formula for pickets:

Pickets = Total length ÷ (Picket width + Gap width)

Fence Style Picket Width Gap Pickets per foot
Privacy (butt-jointed) 5.5" (1×6) 0" 2.18
Privacy (1/4" gap) 5.5" (1×6) 0.25" 2.09
Semi-privacy 5.5" (1×6) 1" 1.75
Semi-privacy 3.5" (1×4) 1" 2.4
Shadowbox (alternating) 5.5" (1×6) 3" ~2.6 (both sides)

Example (256 ft privacy fence, 1×6 pickets, 1/4" gap):

  • Picket width + gap = 5.5" + 0.25" = 5.75"
  • Pickets per foot = 12 ÷ 5.75 = 2.09
  • Total pickets = 256 × 2.09 = 535 pickets
  • Add 10% waste = 589 pickets

Step 5: Calculate Concrete Needed

Each post needs concrete in its hole. The amount depends on hole depth and diameter:

Fence Height Hole Depth Hole Diameter Bags of Concrete (per post)
4 ft 24" 8" 1-2 bags
6 ft 24-30" 10" 2-3 bags
8 ft 36" 12" 3-4 bags

Rule of thumb: Bury posts 1/3 to 1/2 of the fence height. In cold climates, go below the frost line (36"+) to prevent frost heave.

Example (35 posts, 6 ft fence, 3 bags each):

  • 35 × 3 = 105 bags of concrete

Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. Incorrect Post Spacing

Spacing posts more than 8 ft apart causes sagging rails and loose pickets. Stick to 6-8 ft maximum for wood fences, even if it means buying more posts.

2. Shallow Post Holes

Burying posts too shallow is the #1 reason fences lean or blow over. Always go at least 24" deep (36" in cold climates), and use concrete — not just packed dirt.

3. Not Accounting for Slope

If your yard slopes, you'll need to either step the fence (each section at a different height) or rack it (follow the slope). Stepping requires extra pickets; racking needs adjustable-angle rails.

4. Forgetting Utility Lines

Always call 811 (or your local utility service) before digging. Hitting a gas or water line is dangerous and expensive. It's free and takes 48-72 hours.

5. Mixing Wood Types

Using pressure-treated posts with cedar pickets is fine (they don't touch directly). But mixing cedar and pine pickets in the same fence looks inconsistent and weathers differently. Use one wood type for all visible components.

FAQ - Fence Calculator

How many fence posts do I need for 100 feet?

For 8 ft spacing: 100 ÷ 8 = 12.5 → 13 sections + 1 end post = 14 posts. For 6 ft spacing: 100 ÷ 6 = 16.7 → 17 sections + 1 = 18 posts. Always round up and add 1 for the end post.

How far apart should fence posts be?

Standard wood fence post spacing is 8 feet for normal conditions, or 6 feet for high-wind areas or tall (8 ft) fences. Chain link can go up to 10 ft. Never exceed 10 ft spacing — rails will sag.

How deep should fence posts be buried?

Bury posts 1/3 to 1/2 of the fence height. For a 6 ft fence, bury posts 24-30" deep. In cold climates, go at least 36" deep to get below the frost line and prevent heaving.

How many pickets do I need for 100 feet of privacy fence?

For a privacy fence with 1×6 pickets (5.5" actual width) and 1/4" gap: 100 ft × 12 in/ft ÷ 5.75 in = 208 pickets. Add 10% waste = 229 pickets. For no-gap privacy: 100 × 12 ÷ 5.5 = 218 + 10% = 240 pickets.

How much does a 100 ft wood fence cost?

Materials only: $1,500-2,500 for a 6 ft privacy fence. With professional installation: $2,500-3,200. Cedar costs about 20% more than pressure-treated pine. Add $200-400 for a gate.

Conclusion

Calculating fence materials doesn't have to be a guessing game. By following the steps in this guide — measuring length, spacing posts, counting rails, and calculating pickets — you'll order the right amount every time and avoid costly mistakes.

Quick recap:

  • ✅ Measure total fence length (subtract gate widths)
  • ✅ Calculate posts: (Length ÷ Spacing) + 1, add 2 for gates
  • ✅ Calculate rails: Sections × Rails per section
  • ✅ Calculate pickets: Length ÷ (Picket width + Gap)
  • ✅ Calculate concrete: 2-3 bags per post (6 ft fence)
  • ✅ Add 5-10% waste buffer to everything
  • ✅ Call 811 before you dig!

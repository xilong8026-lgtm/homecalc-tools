Planning a concrete project? Whether you're pouring a small patio, a walkway, or a driveway, accurate concrete estimation is crucial. Order too little, and you'll have to stop mid-pour (which creates a weak seam). Order too much, and you've wasted money on unused concrete.

In this comprehensive guide, you'll learn how to calculate concrete needed for any project. We'll cover measurements, bag yields, ready-mix vs. bagged concrete, slab thickness recommendations, and cost estimation. Plus, we'll show you how to use our free concrete calculator to save time and avoid mistakes.

💡 Pro Tip: Always order 5-10% more concrete than your calculations say. Concrete is heavy and expensive to return, but running out mid-pour is a disaster. The delivery truck won't wait while you run to the store!

Why Accurate Concrete Estimation Matters

Concrete is sold by the cubic yard (ready-mix) or by the bag (80lb, 60lb, or 40lb bags). If you underestimate, you'll have to order more — which may incur delivery fees or require you to rent a mixer again. If you overestimate, you're stuck with heavy, unused bags or paid for concrete that was never poured.

Here's why accurate estimation is critical:

  • Avoid cold joints: A "cold joint" occurs when fresh concrete is poured against concrete that has already started to set. This creates a weak seam that can crack. Accurate estimation ensures you have enough concrete to finish the job in one pour.
  • Save money: Ready-mix concrete costs $120-180 per cubic yard (delivered). Bagged concrete costs $4-7 per 80lb bag. Ordering the right amount saves hundreds of dollars.
  • Plan labor: Concrete must be poured, leveled, and finished before it sets (usually within 2-4 hours). Knowing how much concrete you need helps you plan the number of helpers required.
  • Avoid delivery fees: Ready-mix suppliers charge $50-150 for failed deliveries or partial loads. Accurate estimation avoids these fees.

Understanding Concrete Measurements

Concrete is measured in cubic yards (for ready-mix) or cubic feet (for bagged concrete). Here's the conversion:

  • 1 cubic yard = 27 cubic feet
  • 1 cubic yard of concrete weighs about 4,000 lbs
  • An 80lb bag yields about 0.6 cubic feet (0.022 cubic yards)
  • A 60lb bag yields about 0.45 cubic feet (0.017 cubic yards)
  • A 40lb bag yields about 0.3 cubic feet (0.011 cubic yards)

Important: These yields are approximate. The actual yield depends on how much water you add (more water = weaker concrete but easier to work with).

Step-by-Step Calculation Guide

1

Measure the Area

Measure the length, width, and depth (thickness) of the area you want to pour. For rectangular slabs, multiply length × width × depth to get cubic feet.

Example: A 10×12 ft slab at 4 inches thick:

  • Length: 10 ft
  • Width: 12 ft
  • Depth: 4 inches = 0.333 ft
  • Volume: 10 × 12 × 0.333 = 40 cubic feet
2

Convert to Cubic Yards (for Ready-Mix)

Divide cubic feet by 27 to get cubic yards.

Calculation: 40 cubic feet ÷ 27 = 1.48 cubic yards.

3

Add Waste Factor

Add 5-10% extra concrete to account for spillage, over-excavation, or slight changes in grade.

Calculation: 1.48 cubic yards × 1.10 (10% waste) = 1.63 cubic yards.

Order: Round up to the nearest 0.25 cubic yard (most suppliers have a minimum order of 1-2 cubic yards). In this case, order 1.75 or 2 cubic yards.

4

Calculate Bags (for Small Projects)

If you're using bagged concrete, divide the total cubic feet by the yield per bag.

Calculation (80lb bags): 40 cubic feet ÷ 0.6 cubic feet/bag = 66.7 → 67 bags.

Add waste: 67 bags × 1.10 = 73.7 → 74 bags.

5

Decide: Bags or Ready-Mix?

Use this rule of thumb:

  • Under 0.5 cubic yards: Use bags (you'll need 20-25 bags per cubic yard).
  • 0.5-1 cubic yards: Either bags or ready-mix (depending on your budget and labor).
  • Over 1 cubic yard: Order ready-mix (it's cheaper and ensures a consistent pour).

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Even experienced DIYers make mistakes when estimating concrete. Here are the most common pitfalls:

1. Not Accounting for Waste

Concrete spillage, over-excavation, and slight changes in grade can add up. Always add 5-10% extra.

2. Forgetting About Subbase Thickness

Concrete should be poured on a compacted gravel subbase (4-6 inches thick). If you excavate 6 inches for the subbase and 4 inches for the concrete, that's 10 inches of depth — not 4 inches. Make sure your measurements are accurate.

3. Underestimating Labor

Concrete Sets quickly (2-4 hours in warm weather). For a 2-car driveway (6 cubic yards), you'll need at least 3-4 helpers to pour, level, and finish before it sets. Don't underestimate the physical labor required.

4. Not Ordering Enough

Running out of concrete mid-pour is a disaster. You'll have to order more (with a delivery fee) and the new concrete won't bond well with the old (creating a cold joint). Always order 5-10% extra.

5. Ignoring Weather Conditions

Concrete cures best at 50-80°F. In hot weather, it sets faster (you have less working time). In cold weather, it takes longer to cure (protect it from freezing for at least 24 hours). Plan your pour around the weather.

Cost Estimation

Once you know how much concrete you need, you can estimate the total cost of your project.

Ready-Mix Concrete Costs

  • Concrete (delivered): $120-180 per cubic yard
  • Delivery fee: $50-150 (waived if you order over 5-10 cubic yards)
  • Short load fee: $50-100 (for orders under 5 cubic yards)
  • Pump truck (optional): $300-600 per day (for hard-to-reach areas)

Total cost for 2 cubic yards: $240-360 (concrete) + $100 (delivery/short load fee) = $340-460.

Bagged Concrete Costs

  • 80lb bags: $4-7 per bag
  • 60lb bags: $3-6 per bag
  • Mixer rental (optional): $50-100 per day

Total cost for 2 cubic yards (using 80lb bags): 90 bags × $5 = $450 (plus mixer rental if needed).

Other Materials

  • Gravel subbase: $20-40 per cubic yard (delivered)
  • Rebar: $0.75-1.50 per foot
  • Wire mesh: $0.15-0.30 per sq ft
  • Concrete sealer: $20-40 per gallon (covers 200-400 sq ft)

Frequently Asked Questions

How many 80lb bags of concrete equal a cubic yard?

One cubic yard of concrete weighs about 4,000 lbs. An 80lb bag yields approximately 0.022 cubic yards (0.6 cubic feet). So you need about 45-50 bags per cubic yard. Always buy 5-10% extra to account for waste and spillage.

How much concrete do I need for a 10x10 slab?

For a 10×10 slab at 4 inches thick: 10 × 10 = 100 sq ft. 100 sq ft × 0.333 ft (4 inches) = 33.3 cubic feet. 33.3 ÷ 27 = 1.23 cubic yards. Add 10% waste = 1.36 cubic yards. That's about 62-68 bags of 80lb concrete.

Should I use bags or order ready-mix concrete?

Use bags for small projects (under 1 cubic yard). Order ready-mix for larger projects (over 1 cubic yard). Ready-mix costs $120-180 per cubic yard (delivered), while bagged concrete costs $150-250 per cubic yard (when you include the cost of bags and mixing labor).

How thick should a concrete slab be?

Walkways/patios: 4 inches. Driveways: 5-6 inches. Garage floors: 4-5 inches. Heavy equipment areas: 6-8 inches. Always check local building codes for requirements in your area.

How long does concrete take to cure?

Concrete reaches 70% strength in 7 days, 90% in 28 days. You can walk on it after 24-48 hours, drive on it after 7 days (light vehicles), and full use after 28 days. Keep it moist during the first 7 days for proper curing.
